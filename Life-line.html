Depression is a common mental health condition that affects how a person feels, thinks, and handles daily activities. It involves a persistent low mood, sadness, or loss of interest in things that once felt enjoyable. It is more than a regular bad day or normal mood changes — depression lasts longer and can impact sleep, energy, motivation, and overall well-being.

What is Anxiety?
Anxiety is a feeling of worry, fear, or tension. It can be triggered by real or imagined threats. When anxiety rises, the body activates its natural fight, flight, or freeze response, which can cause physical symptoms like a racing heart, shaking, or trouble breathing. Everyone feels anxious at times, but anxiety becomes a concern when it is strong, frequent, or affects daily life.
Timer explanation
Measuring how long a panic attack or anxiety spike lasts. Tracking the time spent doing grounding or breathing exercises. Counting calm time after a stressful moment. Monitoring how long it’s been since your last anxiety episode. Encouraging awareness of progress over time.
Guidance section
If you’re having a panic attack Focus on slow, steady breathing (in for 4 seconds, out for 6). Name 5 things you can see, 4 you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste. Remind yourself: “This will pass. I am safe.” Sit or lie down in a comfortable position. Types of anxiety Agoraphobia – fear of places where escape feels difficult. Generalized Anxiety Disorder – constant worry about everyday things. Panic Disorder – sudden, intense waves of fear. Social Anxiety – fear of social situations or being judged. Specific Phobias – intense fear of certain objects or situations. Medical/Substance-related anxiety – anxiety caused by health conditions or substances. Selective Mutism, Separation Anxiety, and others – anxiety in specific situations. Controlling your environment Move to a quieter or familiar space. Dim lights or reduce noise. Keep grounding tools: water, a soft object, calming sounds. Getting help Talk to a mental-health professional if anxiety affects daily life. Reach out to someone you trust for support. If symptoms become overwhelming or unsafe, seek immediate help.

What is PTSD?
Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) is a mental health condition that can develop after experiencing or witnessing a traumatic event. People with PTSD may experience flashbacks, nightmares, strong anxiety, or intrusive thoughts about what happened. Although many people feel distress after trauma, PTSD is diagnosed when symptoms last longer than a month and make everyday life difficult. With support and professional help, people with PTSD can improve and heal.
Timer explanation
Tracking the duration of flashbacks, dissociation, or high-stress moments. Counting how long you’ve been grounded or stable. Monitoring how long it has been since your last triggering event. Measuring the time spent doing grounding or self-soothing. Creating a sense of progress by showing increasing stability periods.
Guidance section
If you’re having flashbacks or intrusive memories Ground yourself: touch a surface, hold something textured, notice your surroundings. Remind yourself: “This is a memory. I am safe right now.” Focus on slow breathing or a calming object. If possible, move to a safe and quiet place. For nightmares or severe anxiety Turn on a light or orient yourself in the present. Practice a grounding routine or calming activity. Reach out to someone you trust if you need support. Controlling your environment Keep your space safe, calm, and predictable. Use soft lighting, familiar items, and comforting scents. Avoid sudden noises or overwhelming triggers when possible. Getting help PTSD is treatable with professional support (therapy, trauma-focused care). If you feel unsafe or overwhelmed, reach out to crisis professionals. Healing takes time — and you don’t have to do it alone.

What is an Eating Disorder?
Eating disorders are serious conditions that affect a person’s relationship with food, body image, and emotions. They involve challenging thoughts and behaviors around eating and can impact physical, mental, and social health. Common types include anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, binge eating disorder, ARFID, OSFED, pica, and rumination disorder. Eating disorders are treatable, and recovery is possible with the right support.
Timer explanation
Tracking how long it has been since harmful urges or episodes. Measuring the duration of coping activities. Monitoring time since your last difficult moment or strong thought. Tracking streaks toward recovery. Building consistency with routines or support practices.
Guidance section
Types of eating disorders Anorexia Nervosa – severe restriction of food and fear of weight gain. Bulimia Nervosa – cycles of bingeing and compensatory behaviors. Binge Eating Disorder – recurring episodes of consuming large amounts of food. ARFID – limited intake due to sensory issues or fear of consequences. OSFED – disordered eating that doesn’t fully match other categories. Controlling your environment Try to maintain a safe and structured routine around meals. Avoid triggering media or conversations about body weight. Surround yourself with supportive people when possible. Keep items that comfort you nearby. Getting help Eating disorders are serious medical conditions — professional care is important. Reach out to a doctor, therapist, or nutrition specialist. If you feel physically unwell or in danger, seek immediate medical help.

Let your body know it’s safe.” “You deserve comfort and compassion, not criticism.” “One step at a time is enough. Healing takes time.”

What is ADHD?
ADHD (Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder) is a neurodevelopmental condition that affects attention, motivation, and self-regulation. People with ADHD may experience difficulties with focus, restlessness, organizing tasks, or controlling impulses. ADHD often begins in childhood but can continue into adulthood. With understanding, structure, and support, people with ADHD can learn strategies that help them thrive in daily life.
Timer explanation
Tracking how long you’ve been focused on a task Monitoring the time spent on routines or activities Measuring time between distractions Helping with awareness of time passing (time-blindness support) Recording streaks of completed focus sessions
Guidance section
Improving inattention Break tasks into small steps. Use timers or reminders to stay on track. Keep your environment organized and minimal. Improving hyperactivity Let yourself move: stretching, pacing, fidget tools. Take short breaks during tasks. Use calming activities like deep breathing or music. Improving impulsivity Pause and count to 5 before reacting when you can. Keep a notepad or digital board to jot down thoughts instead of acting immediately. Practice routines that support planning and structure. Controlling your environment Reduce distractions (noise, clutter, screens). Use visual supports: calendars, checklists, color coding. Create a calm, predictable workspace. Getting help ADHD professionals can help with diagnosis, strategies, and treatment. Support groups, coaching, or therapy can improve daily functioning. If ADHD is affecting school, work, or relationships, reaching out for help can make a big difference.
Support scripts/Gentle reminders/Encouragements
Gentle reminders “Your brain works differently — not wrongly.” “It’s okay to take breaks.” “You don’t need to be perfect to be worthy.” “Small actions count — one step is still progress.” “You’re trying, and that matters.” Encouragement “Your creativity and energy are strengths.” “You can learn new ways to manage things. It’s not too late.” “You’re capable of growth — even when it feels messy.” “Celebrate the little wins. They add up.” Supportive scripts “Take one small task and start gently.” “You don’t have to do everything at once.” “It’s okay to ask for structure and support — it helps.”

What is Anxiety?
Anxiety is a feeling of worry, fear, or tension. It can be triggered by real or imagined threats. When anxiety rises, the body activates its natural fight, flight, or freeze response, which can cause physical symptoms like a racing heart, shaking, or trouble breathing. Everyone feels anxious at times, but anxiety becomes a concern when it is strong, frequent, or affects daily life.

If you’re having a panic attack Focus on slow, steady breathing (in for 4 seconds, out for 6). Name 5 things you can see, 4 you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste. Selective Mutism, Separation Anxiety, and others – anxiety in specific situations. Controlling your environment Move to a quieter or familiar space. Dim lights or reduce noise. Keep grounding tools: water, a soft object, calming sounds. Getting help Talk to a mental-health professional if anxiety affects daily life. Reach out to someone you trust for support. What is PTSD?
Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) is a mental health condition that can develop after experiencing or witnessing a traumatic event. People with PTSD may experience flashbacks, nightmares, strong anxiety, or intrusive thoughts about what happened. Although many people feel distress after trauma, PTSD is diagnosed when symptoms last longer than a month and make everyday life difficult. With support and professional help, people with PTSD can improve and heal.
Timer explanation
Tracking the duration of flashbacks, dissociation, or high-stress moments. Counting how long you’ve been grounded or stable. Monitoring how long it has been since your last triggering event. Measuring the time spent doing grounding or self-soothing. Creating a sense of progress by showing increasing stability periods.
Guidance section
If you’re having flashbacks or intrusive memories Ground yourself: touch a surface, hold something textured, notice your surroundings. Remind yourself: “This is a memory. I am safe right now.” Focus on slow breathing or a calming object. If possible, move to a safe and quiet place. For nightmares or severe anxiety Turn on a light or orient yourself in the present. Practice a grounding routine or calming activity. Reach out to someone you trust if you need support. Controlling your environment Keep your space safe, calm, and predictable. Use soft lighting, familiar items, and comforting scents. Avoid sudden noises or overwhelming triggers when possible. Getting help PTSD is treatable with professional support (therapy, trauma-focused care). If you feel unsafe or overwhelmed, reach out to crisis professionals. Healing takes time — and you don’t have to do it alone.

What is an Eating Disorder?
Eating disorders are serious conditions that affect a person’s relationship with food, body image, and emotions. They involve challenging thoughts and behaviors around eating and can impact physical, mental, and social health. Common types include anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, binge eating disorder, ARFID, OSFED, pica, and rumination disorder. Eating disorders are treatable, and recovery is possible with the right support.
Timer explanation

Guidance section

OSFED – disordered eating that doesn’t fully match other categories. Controlling your environment Try to maintain a safe and structured routine around meals. Getting help Eating disorders are serious medical conditions — professional care is important. Reach out to a doctor, therapist, or nutrition specialist. If you feel physically unwell or in danger, seek immediate medical help.

What is ADHD?
ADHD (Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder) is a neurodevelopmental condition that affects attention, motivation, and self-regulation. People with ADHD may experience difficulties with focus, restlessness, organizing tasks, or controlling impulses. ADHD often begins in childhood but can continue into adulthood. With understanding, structure, and support, people with ADHD can learn strategies that help them thrive in daily life.
Timer explanation
Tracking how long you’ve been focused on a task Monitoring the time spent on routines or activities Measuring time between distractions Helping with awareness of time passing (time-blindness support) Recording streaks of completed focus sessions
Guidance section
Improving inattention Break tasks into small steps. Use timers or reminders to stay on track. Keep your environment organized and minimal. Improving hyperactivity Let yourself move: stretching, pacing, fidget tools. Take short breaks during tasks. Use calming activities like deep breathing or music. Improving impulsivity Pause and count to 5 before reacting when you can. Keep a notepad or digital board to jot down thoughts instead of acting immediately. Practice routines that support planning and structure. Controlling your environment Reduce distractions (noise, clutter, screens). Use visual supports: calendars, checklists, color coding. Create a calm, predictable workspace. Getting help ADHD professionals can help with diagnosis, strategies, and treatment. Support groups, coaching, or therapy can improve daily functioning. If ADHD is affecting school, work, or relationships, reaching out for help can make a big difference.
